Scenario 1 of 8

The Jackpot SMS

You just received this text message. It claims you won $50,000 in a lottery you don't remember entering!

Is this text message safe to click?

🚩 Scan Red Flags Revealed:
  • Unknown Sender: Official banks or lotteries do not text from random numbers.
  • Unsolicited Wins: You cannot win a lottery you never bought a ticket for.
  • Suspicious URL: The link ends in `.xyz` which is commonly used by scammers.
How this scam works:

The link leads to a copy of a banking website. When you enter your card details to "deposit the winnings", scammers harvest your card details and empty your account.

🛡️ Safety Tips:
  • Never open links from numbers you do not recognize.
  • Delete and block the sender.
  • Report the number to your local carrier or government cybercell (e.g., 7726 in many countries).
{% elif scenario_id == 'email-bank' %}
Scenario 2 of 8

The Suspicious Email

An email warning of an account suspension arrives. The sender demands validation within 24 hours to secure your funds.

What is the safest action here?

🚩 Scan Red Flags Revealed:
  • Fake Sender Address: The domain `securebank-support.net` is a mockup created by hackers.
  • Generic Salutation: "Dear Customer" instead of your actual name.
  • High-Urgency Threat: Threatening suspension within 24 hours creates fear.
How this scam works:

The "Restore Account" button redirects to a credential harvester page. When you input your username and password, the scammer receives them instantly, allows them to log into your account, and wires away your savings.

🛡️ Safety Tips:
  • Never click login buttons within unsolicited emails.
  • Check the sender address carefully for typos or odd endings.
  • Always type your bank's website manually into the address bar.
{% elif scenario_id == 'wa-friend' %}
Scenario 3 of 8

The New-Number Friend

An online text from your close friend claims they lost their phone and desperately need money to cover an emergency invoice.

Is it safe to wire the money immediately?

🚩 Scan Red Flags Revealed:
  • Sudden Number Swap: Texting from a brand-new number claiming they lost their phone.
  • Panic Trigger: An urgent request for money is designed to bypass your caution.
  • Suspicious Method: Pushing for immediate online wire transfers.
How this scam works:

Scammers download pictures from social media, look up friends' contacts via leaked databases, and set up duplicate accounts. By posing as the friend in distress, they exploit your kindness to steal quick cash.

🛡️ Safety Tips:
  • Never send money based on text messages alone.
  • Always call the original phone number to confirm it's actually them.
  • Set a secret family/friend password or ask a question only your actual friend would know.
{% elif scenario_id == 'dm-copyright' %}
Scenario 4 of 8

The Account Warning

An account claiming to be Instagram Support sends you a direct message warning that you have violated copyright policies, threating immediate account deletion.

Would you trust this support link?

🚩 Scan Red Flags Revealed:
  • In-box Warnings: Official help centers use account notifications or email, never chats.
  • Lookalike URL: The domain is `help-instagram.net` instead of the official platform domain.
  • Immediate Deletion: Instilling panic (24 hours) is a classic phishing strategy.
How this scam works:

The link leads to a page styled like a portal. When you enter your email, password, and two-factor authentication code, scammers take control of your profile, lock you out, and target your connections.

🛡️ Safety Tips:
  • If you receive a policy warning, check your app Settings -> Help -> Support Requests.
  • Never enter passwords on external links received in chats.
  • Set up multi-factor verification (2FA) via authentication apps for security.
{% elif scenario_id == 'scareware' %}
Scenario 5 of 8

The Virus Warning

While reading news articles online, your screen flashes with an urgent alert warning you that your computer is heavily infected by spyware.

What is the safest action here?

🚩 Scan Red Flags Revealed:
  • Browser Origin: Real security tools never run scans inside browser pages.
  • Blinking Alarms: Dramatic alerts are scare tactics to prevent logical reasoning.
  • Support Hotline: Demanding phone assistance is typical of support scams.
How this scam works:

This is "Scareware". Clicking install installs malware, or calling the number links you to fake technicians. They gain remote computer access, install spyware, and demand credit card payments.

🛡️ Safety Tips:
  • Instantly close pop-ups by pressing `Ctrl + W` (Windows) or `Cmd + W` (Mac).
  • Keep system firewalls and browsers updated to block these threats automatically.
  • Rely only on local antivirus programs installed directly on your machine.
{% elif scenario_id == 'wifi-snare' %}
Scenario 6 of 8

The Free Wi-Fi Snare

Waiting for your plane at the airport, you see an open network named "Free_Airport_WiFi_Secure". You connect to it and want to quickly check your bank balance.

Is it safe to log into your banking app?

🚩 Scan Red Flags Revealed:
  • No Padlock Lock: It does not require a password, meaning all traffic is open.
  • Fake Trust Label: Adding "Secure" to the name is an easy way to trick users.
How this scam works:

Hackers run "Evil Twin" Wi-Fi networks in airports or cafes. Once connected, they intercept everything you browse, and they grab passwords, credit card inputs, and session cookies.

🛡️ Safety Tips:
  • Disable automated Wi-Fi connection configurations on devices.
  • Use cellular data (3G/4G/5G) or a Virtual Private Network (VPN) for sensitive tasks.
  • Always confirm the cafe/airport official network credentials with personnel.
{% elif scenario_id == 'job-scam' %}
Scenario 7 of 8

The Too-Easy Job

An unsolicited text message from an unknown "HR Manager" offers an easy work-from-home job that guarantees hundreds of dollars per day for simple video likes.

Is this a real job opportunity?

🚩 Scan Red Flags Revealed:
  • Extreme Salary: Offering $600/day for clicks is financially impossible.
  • Random Recruiters: HR teams do not reach out on personal messaging apps.
  • Push to Apps: Redirecting to Telegram or other apps to initiate work.
How this scam works:

This is a "Task Scam". They pay you $5 or $10 first to win your trust. Then, they require you to buy VIP upgrade packages or deposit money to unlock larger payouts, which they pocket before disabling your profile.

🛡️ Safety Tips:
  • If a career opportunity sounds too simple and pays too well, it is fake.
  • Never send money to an employer to start working or withdraw earnings.
  • Block the account immediately.
{% elif scenario_id == 'grandparent-call' %}
Scenario 8 of 8

The Urgent Phone Call

A call comes in from a name listed as "GRANDSON?". The voice sounds like your grandson crying, saying he's in jail and needs immediate bail money wired.

What is the first thing you should do?

🚩 Scan Red Flags Revealed:
  • Request for Secrecy: Demanding you "do not tell mom" is used to prevent you from discovering they are safe.
  • Wire Payment Methods: Requesting wires, gift cards, or crypto bail payments.
  • Spoofed Voice/Caller ID: Using voice clips from social media or spoofing cell numbers.
How this scam works:

This is the "Grandparent Scam". Fraudsters scrape details from social media or use AI voice tools to clone a grandchild's voice. They trigger heavy anxiety to bypass reasoning, making victims pay fake bail collectors.

🛡️ Safety Tips:
  • Always hang up and verify. Phone numbers and voices can be forged.
  • Create a secret family "passcode word" to confirm identity during family emergencies.
  • Never send funds via wire transfer, reloadable cards, or cryptocurrency to strangers.
