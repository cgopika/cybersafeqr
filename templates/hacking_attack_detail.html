5. Legal Awareness

Website defacement is a punishable cybercrime under the Information Technology Act, 2000. Unauthorized changes to a website or its content can violate Indian cyber laws and lead to legal consequences.

⚖️

Information Technology Act, 2000

The main Indian law that covers unauthorized access, interference with computer systems, and cyber offences involving digital platforms.

📜

Section 43

Covers unauthorized access, damage to computer systems, or alteration of data without permission.

🛡️

Section 66

Punishes dishonest or fraudulent acts under Section 43. Penalties may include up to 3 years imprisonment, a fine of ₹5 lakh, or both.

🔐

Section 66C

Addresses identity theft involving stolen usernames, passwords, or other digital credentials.

🧑‍💻

Section 66D

Covers cheating by personation using computer resources, including using a defaced website to deceive users.

🏛️

Section 65

Punishes knowingly altering, destroying, or hiding required computer source code. Punishment can include up to 3 years imprisonment, a ₹2 lakh fine, or both.

⚠️ "Website defacement is a serious cybercrime. Never access, modify, or test a website without explicit authorization. Unauthorized actions can result in legal penalties under Indian cyber laws."

5. Legal Awareness

Launching a Denial of Service attack is illegal under Indian law and can trigger civil and criminal penalties depending on the intent and target.

📜

Section 43

Unauthorized disruption or damage to computer systems may lead to compensation liability under the IT Act.

🛡️

Section 66

A DoS attack committed dishonestly or fraudulently is a criminal offence punishable with imprisonment and/or fine.

🔥

Section 66F

If the attack targets critical infrastructure or threatens national security, it may be treated as cyber terrorism.

🏛️

Section 351, BNS 2023

This section applies when the attack is used to threaten or intimidate an individual or organization.
