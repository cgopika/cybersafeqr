Case File

The Missing Rs. 50,000

Monday 9:15 AM

Priya checked her online bank statement and noticed a payment of Rs. 50,000 she never made. Concerned, she immediately contacted her bank. An investigation revealed that malware had altered her banking records before the transaction was processed, making the payment appear legitimate. Fortunately, the bank detected the tampering, reversed the transaction, and restored the correct records. Although the money was recovered, the incident caused financial stress and delayed important payments.

Key Lesson: Never ignore unexpected changes in digital data. Verifying information early can help prevent fraud, confusion, and financial or personal loss.

Where Data Can Be Altered

Banking RecordsAccount balance and payment details can be changed.
Medical RecordsHealth data may be altered to hide or add conditions.
Student MarksGrades and scores can be changed in the database.
Business FilesContracts and invoices may be tampered with.
Website ContentPages and forms can display false information.
Government DocumentsOfficial records can be modified without notice.

Protect Yourself

  • Keep regular backups
  • Enable two-factor authentication
  • Limit edit permissions
  • Verify important records
  • Update software regularly
  • Monitor file changes

Legal Framework

Section 43 – Information Technology Act, 2000

Unauthorized alteration, deletion, modification, or damage to electronic data or computer systems is a civil offence and may result in compensation liability.

Section 66 – Information Technology Act, 2000

Altering or damaging computer data dishonestly or fraudulently is a criminal offence punishable with imprisonment of up to 3 years, a fine, or both.

Section 65 – Information Technology Act, 2000

Knowingly tampering with, concealing, modifying, or destroying computer source code required by law is punishable with up to 3 years imprisonment, a fine up to ₹2 lakh, or both.

Unauthorized modification, deletion, or manipulation of electronic data is illegal and may attract both civil liability and criminal prosecution under the Information Technology Act, 2000.

Always verify important documents before trusting or sharing them. A small change in data can have a big impact.
